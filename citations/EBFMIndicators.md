    Title: Quantitative ecosystem indicators for fisheries management

# Overview

A summary of articles presented in an ICES special issue [62(3)](http://icesjms.oxfordjournals.org/content/62/3.toc) on Quantitative Ecosystem Indicators for Fisheries Management. Articles are the result of a symposium focusing on using ecosystem indicators for fisheries management.

Indicators should be meaningful and accurately reflect the state of the ecosystem. Ecosystems involve both target and non-target species as well as the habitat in which these species live, and indicators should take all aspects into consideration. Therefore, management interventions will necessarily involve trade-offs. No single indicator can represent the entire system; therefore, a suite of indicators will be necessary. Furthermore, the ability of indicators to represent the true dynamics may be hampered by ecosystem, history of exploitation, or other pressures.

# Indicators

## Ability

The ability of an indicator to synthesize the structure and function of an ecosystem in both time and space, and how fisheries and their actions influence the indicator. Indicators typically quantify (i) climate change, environmental variability, or fishing and their effects on the ecosystem (ii) the functioning of the exploited systems such as species- and age-composition (iii) impact of fishing on all species present

## Use

Evaluation of indicators relative to management objectives, or the on the ground use of indicators. A single indicator has limited use, and instead frameworks must be established to combine indicators to give a complete picture of the ecosystem. Furthermore, indicators can not just be evaluated, they must also be communicated to managers and the public.

## Types

### Trophodynamic

Several trophodynamic indicators are needed to capture change in the ecosystem. Choices regarding the assignment of trophic level have serious affects on the outcome.

#### Low-trophic-level indicators

Capture environmental change and bottom-up effects, but do not capture change in higher trophic levels.

### Size-based

Size-based indicators are informative about overexploitation.

### Community-level

Community-level indicators are often thought of as a holistic measure of change, but they require more comprehensive data than population-level indicators and they are increasingly sensitive to short-term fluctuations and species-specific impacts;

### Population-based

Population-based indicators are relatively easy to measure and understand, often respond in predictable ways, sensitive to changes in the ecosystem, and are thought to be applicable across ecosystems (<span class="citeproc-not-found" data-reference-id="Brodziak2002">**???**</span>, (<span class="citeproc-not-found" data-reference-id="Rochet2003">**???**</span>)).

Fishing has far-reaching affects and can often result in changes to the ecosystem beyond those fish species that are targeted. Survey data offers an ample source to judge the effects of fishing on species other than those targeted by fishermen, which often have complicated stock assessments that monitor their status. Trends in non-targeted species can provide indicators for the impacts of fishing on the community, and if trends are similar across targeted and non-target species then one can conclude that the trends are due to more than just exploitation.
