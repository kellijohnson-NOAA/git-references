# Summary
An *ex ante* analysis of the potential for an ITQ program in the
US West Coast fixed-gear sablefish fishery.

# Introduction

## ITQ
It is assumed that some owners will choose to sell their quota and 
leave the fishery, thereby decreasing overcapitalization.
Vessels may choose not to exit because of small resale prices.
Here, an ITQ was considered to reduce overcapitalization,
decrease contention about allocation, and solve various other problems.

## US West Coast fixed-gear sablefish fishery
Until 1970 with the rise of an export market for sablefish to Japan,
the sablefish fishery was underdeveloped. 
In 1979 the pot fleet grew from 60 to 207 vessels. 
In 1980 the fleet decreased when the price-per-pound dropped sharply,
and then remained relatively small while the ex-vessel price doubled 
over 1981-1988, with just 26 vessels in 1988.
When using longline gear, sablefish are often harvested with rockfish, halibut, and lingcod. 

## Model
Theoretical model of economics derived from implementing an ITQ program
are described below. Quotas were allocated to vessels according to their
historical production. It should be noted that in actuality quotas were
assigned to vessels based on a tier system and not in proportion to their
historical production, but this analysis was done before the policy was implemented.
Demand for quota was driven by both demand from an individual vessel, 
based on species-specific quota available, output prices, vessel costs,
and vessel production technology, and market demand, based on the sum
of all individual vessel-derived demand curves. 

## Results
The gains from trade, of 12.60\%, decline as the number of vessels entering the market increases. The most efficient and least efficient vessels benefit
the most from trading because the most efficient vessels will buy out the least
efficient. 
Flexibility to change species acquisition to that for which vessels can obtain 
quota for may be different than what a vessel can catch because of bycatch, even in a fixed-gear fishery. Therefore, there may be less scope to adjust
production to trade than what can be done in the longline fishery with less bycatch.
Limited alternatives available to vessels may cause the marginal valuation of
a firm's capital to remain above its opportunity costs causing severe disequilibrium. Therefore, causing vessels to remain in the fishery, even
if it is not economically viable for them to do so.
